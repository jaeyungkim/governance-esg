    When the Machinery Breaks

    African Bank, and the anatomy of a governance failure.
    Jae Yung Kim29 June 2026≈ 22 min read

    This morning we built the governance machine — boards, votes, blockholders, incentives — and watched it carry environmental and social preferences into what a company actually does. This afternoon we watch the same machine fail. African Bank went from “South African Bank of the Year” to seizure by the central bank in eighteen months, wiping out some thirteen thousand small investors. No fraud was ever found. The collapse was something more instructive than a crime: a governance failure, assembled lever by lever out of the very mechanisms we studied this morning. This reading builds that failure as the board itself experienced it — six decisions, each a lesson — and ends where ESG begins, in the social wreckage a broken board leaves behind.

    Prologue

    “Bank of the Year,” and then nothing

    In November 2012, The Banker — the Financial Times group's magazine for the banking industry — named African Bank its “South African Bank of the Year.” The citation praised a “robust business model” and “sophisticated risk models.” It was an unremarkable award to give. The holding company, African Bank Investments Limited — ABIL — had been one of the hottest stocks on the Johannesburg exchange for a decade. Its profits had climbed from under a billion rand to R3.1bn; its share price had risen more than eight-fold; its founder-chief-executive was celebrated as the man who had taught South Africa how to lend to the poor. By every visible measure it was a success, and its governance, on paper, was exemplary: an independent-majority board, a full slate of committees, a former audit-firm partner as chief financial officer.

    Before you read on, it is worth committing to a guess, because the whole point of the case is that the obvious answer is wrong.

    Consider this

    A bank goes from “Bank of the Year” to seizure by the central bank in eighteen months. What is the most likely root cause — fraud, a bad strategy, poor risk management, a failed board, or simply bad luck? Hold your answer; we will return to it at the very end.

    Here is what happened. By August 2014 the shares were worth less than one rand — down over 90% in a single week. The South African Reserve Bank seized the bank and placed it under curatorship. And roughly thirteen thousand small, mostly black investors — many of them the bank's own employees and customers, who had bought in through community share schemes — were wiped out entirely. The most striking fact in the entire case is the one the official investigation reported a year later: there was no fraud. Nobody stole anything. The bank collapsed not because someone broke the law but because the people charged with governing it did their jobs badly. That is what makes African Bank worth two hours of a finance class rather than a paragraph in a true-crime book.

    When governance fails, it does not merely lower returns. It manufactures catastrophe — and hands the bill to the people least able to pay it.

    This is the thesis that carries over from the morning, turned upside down. We argued there that governance is the transmission mechanism: the machinery through which the preferences of a firm's owners reach the firm's decisions. In the lecture we watched that machinery deliver environmental and social outcomes. Here we watch the identical machinery — a dominant chief executive, a deferential board, an unqualified risk officer, a buried conflict of interest — transmit ruin instead. We will build the failure the way the board built it: one decision at a time, each one a lever from the morning's toolkit, each one pulled the wrong way.

    Part One

    The company that did one thing

    To understand why African Bank was so fragile, you have to understand how unusual it was. It was not a bank in the ordinary sense at all. It did exactly one thing, for one kind of customer, with one kind of money — and it did that one thing on an enormous scale.

    African Bank made unsecured loans: cash loans with no collateral behind them, to mostly lower-income South Africans, for the ordinary emergencies of a hard life — a car repair, a funeral, school fees, a medical bill, the settling of an older debt. The business had its roots in the deliberate exclusions of apartheid. Most poor, black South Africans had been kept from owning property, so they had no title deeds to pledge against a loan and no access to mainstream credit; a 1992 legal exemption created room for small unsecured loans as a regulated alternative to loan sharks. African Bank built itself in that space, and by 2013 it dominated it. It had around three million customers, more than five thousand staff, over fifteen hundred outlets, and a loan book of R59bn — more than thirty percent of the country's entire unsecured-lending market.

    Its own auditor coined the term that best describes it. African Bank, the auditor wrote, was a mono-line bank: “it offers and earns monies from only one product — unsecured loan finance.” It took no customer deposits. It ran no current accounts, no mortgages, no savings products, no other line of any kind. Where a normal bank is a diversified portfolio of activities, African Bank was a single, undiversified bet, repeated three million times.

    Funded by markets, not by depositors

    The second unusual thing about African Bank was where its money came from, and this is the detail that turned fragility into mortality. A normal bank funds its lending with customer deposits — sticky, granular, insured money that tends to stay put even in bad times. African Bank had no deposits to speak of. It funded itself instead by selling bonds — wholesale debt — to a small number of large institutions. Roughly seventy percent of its funding came from long-term notes and barely one percent from deposits; a comparable but far healthier peer, Capitec, drew more than three-quarters of its funding from deposits.

    Concentration made it worse still. Around ten institutions supplied more than three-quarters of the bank's funding, and a shareholder — the asset manager Coronation — had flagged this explicitly as a risk: wholesale funding is confidence-sensitive, and confidence can vanish in a week. A bank that lends long and borrows short from a handful of nervous institutions is a bank that can survive only as long as those institutions keep believing in it. That belief was the whole foundation, and it was thinner than it looked.

    2008: a furniture chain, and a foreshadowing

    In 2008 African Bank did the one big thing it ever did outside its single product line: it paid R9.1bn for Ellerine Holdings, a furniture retailer with more than a thousand stores and nearly eight thousand employees. The strategic logic was to plant African Bank loan kiosks inside the furniture stores and grow the customer base from there. But notice the governance detail, because it returns as the sharpest moment in the case: by one account the chief executive sought regulatory approval for the acquisition before his own board had approved it. The board convened five days later — nearly half of the directors joining by teleconference — and signed off after his presentation. The deal was, in effect, presented to the board as done.

    Remember Ellerines
    The furniture business will reappear twice — first as the source of the bank's largest and most conflicted loan, and then, in August 2014, as the domino that falls first. Keep it in view.

    Who was in the room

    On paper, the board that oversaw all of this was a model of good governance. As of September 2013 it had eleven directors — four executive and seven independent non-executive — and five committees: audit, risk, ethics, remuneration, and directors' affairs. An independent majority, a full committee structure, the textbook architecture. The problem was never the architecture. It was the people inside it, and the way power actually flowed among them.

    That table is the case in miniature. Seven independent directors and five committees did not prevent any of what follows, because governance is not the chart of who sits where. It is the behaviour those people actually engage in. We turn now to six decisions in which the behaviour failed.

    Part Two

    Six failures, one missing thing

    What follows is not a list of villains. It is a sequence of ordinary board decisions, each defensible in the moment, each a textbook governance mechanism that was present in form and absent in force. As you read, ask the question the directors should have asked — and notice how often the honest answer was “nothing was stopping us, and no one did.”

    Failure 1 · board independenceThe founder who was always right

    For almost the whole of African Bank's history, the official investigation later found, “there was no material decision … which did not carry [Kirkinis's] support.” He was, in the report's phrase, “the person who had to be persuaded.” No other director knew the bank as intimately; he had built it. When he and other directors disagreed, the board “gave more credence to his views.” Put yourself in the position of an independent director on that board. The co-founder has been right for twenty years. He owns the room. The share price says he is a genius.

    Consider this

    What can a board actually do to keep a dominant, successful, founder-chief-executive accountable — when every instinct, and every quarter's results, tells the directors to defer to him?

    The investigation's answer was unsparing. It used the word hubris: Kirkinis tended to believe that “he was right and everyone else was wrong,” and the board, it concluded, was “dominated by Mr Kirkinis.” One analyst put the structural version plainly: “CEOs can become too big for their boards … the balance of power sits with the CEO and the board feels it cannot really challenge him.” A newspaper headline was blunter still — “Board beguiled by toxic charm.” The nuance worth keeping is that a handful of directors did push back; the report names a few who raised objections. Independence, in other words, was not uniformly absent. It was simply too weak, in too few people, to move a board that had spent two decades agreeing with one man.

    The lever · board independence & the CEO–board balance

    Seven independent directors and a full committee structure did not produce independent challenge. Independence is not a box on an org chart; it is a behaviour — the willingness to say no to someone who has been right for twenty years. Structure is necessary, but it is never sufficient. This is precisely the gap between a board's form and its function that the morning's work on boards as monitors was about.

    Failure 2 · the risk functionWho owned the risk?

    Consider how the riskiest loan book in the country was supervised. The bank's Chief Risk Officer was a trained botanist and former civil servant with no banking background, who in the crucial crisis years was, the investigation found, frequently incapacitated by a severe drinking problem and able to make “no meaningful contribution.” The chief executive knew, and eventually put him on extended leave — but he remained a director of the bank. And in March 2013, at the most important moment, the chief financial officer recommended an additional R450m bad-debt provision; the chief executive thought R350m was enough — “the second half is always better” — and the chief executive prevailed.

    Consider this

    What is a board risk committee actually for? Whose job was it to stop an unqualified risk officer and an optimistic chief executive from setting aside too little for losses — and why did that safeguard not fire?

    The provisioning episode is the tell, and it is worth slowing down on. A bad-debt provision is the bank's own estimate of how much of its loan book will not be repaid. When the chief executive can overrule the chief financial officer on how large the losses are, reported profit stops being a fact and becomes a matter of opinion — and the opinion that wins is the one that flatters the results and postpones the reckoning. A risk function exists to be the one voice in the building that is structurally permitted to be pessimistic. At African Bank that voice had neither the expertise nor the standing to be heard over the founder's optimism.

    The lever · board competence & the risk function

    A risk committee disciplines management only if it has both the expertise to know what it is looking at and the standing to say no and make it stick. Here it had neither — an unqualified officer, an overridden provision, a profit number that had become an opinion. Competence on the board is not a courtesy; it is the precondition for oversight to mean anything.

    Failure 3 · strategy oversightOne product, one bet

    We have already seen that African Bank was a mono-line lender by description. What matters for governance is that it was mono-line by choice, against repeated advice. The bank was urged, more than once, to diversify into other banking services that might steady it through a downturn. The chief executive refused; for him, other products were a “no-go area.” That refusal is the crux of the case's hardest discussion, because diversification is normally management's call, not the board's — and yet concentration this extreme had stopped being a strategy choice and become an existential risk.

    Consider this

    Diversification is usually management's prerogative. But when concentration becomes a threat to survival, should the board overrule the chief executive and force the firm to diversify? Where, exactly, is the line between respecting strategy and protecting the firm?

    Reality answered the question for them. In 2012 a wave of platinum-mining strikes tore through the bank's borrower base — the very lower-income workers it lent to — and defaults jumped from roughly ten times to thirty times the levels a normal bank would see. A diversified bank absorbs a sector shock like that somewhere else on its balance sheet. African Bank had no “somewhere else.” With no other business lines to cushion the blow, it posted, in 2013, the first loss in its history: R4.2bn.

    The lever · strategy oversight & challenge

    The board's clearest single failure was not stopping a concentration it had been warned about. A diversified bank survives a strike season; a mono-line bank built on one product, one borrower segment, and one funding source is the downturn when it comes. Overseeing strategy means challenging the bet, not just admiring the returns it produces while the weather holds.

    Failure 4 · conflicts of interestLending to your own subsidiary

    Now Ellerines returns. By 2014 African Bank had lent R1.4bn to Ellerines — its own struggling furniture subsidiary, which by then needed at least R70m a month simply to keep operating. Hold two structural facts together. First, Toni Fourie ran Ellerines and sat on the bank's board. Second, the bank and its holding company shared the same directors, meeting on the same day — what the case calls a “unitary board.” So a bank whose entire business was small loans to individuals made a very large loan to a failing furniture retailer that it happened to own, with the retailer's chief executive in the room.

    Consider this

    Whose interests was the board serving when it approved those loans — the bank's, whose depositors and bondholders bore the risk, or the holding company's, which needed to keep its furniture business alive?

    The investigation's verdict on this point is the most quotable in the case. “No reasonable banker,” it found, would have made the Ellerines loans; in making them, the directors “acted in breach of their fiduciary duty … to the bank.” The conflict is structural and clean: what is good for ABIL, the parent — prop up the furniture business it owns — is not what is good for the bank, which should never have lent R1.4bn to a failing retailer. A unitary board, with the same people wearing both hats, could not hold the two apart. The most telling detail of all: when asked about the conflict afterward, every director but one denied that it had even existed.

    “No reasonable banker” would have made the Ellerines loans.— Myburgh Report, South African Reserve Bank, 2016
    The lever · conflicts of interest & related-party transactions

    Group structures manufacture divided loyalties. A bank director's fiduciary duty runs to the bank — to its depositors and its bondholders — not to the parent company's other ventures. The unitary board obscured exactly the conflict the rules on related-party transactions exist to expose, and a director who cannot see a conflict is in no position to manage it.

    Failure 5 · audit independenceHow independent was the audit?

    The system's last line of defence against a too-rosy picture is supposed to be the audit — the auditors and the audit committee whose job is to tell the uncomfortable truth. At African Bank that line was compromised from both ends. The chief financial officer, Nalliah, was a former Deloitte partner, and ABIL had been his client; Deloitte was the bank's external auditor. Meanwhile the chair of the audit committee, Sithole, complained that management's reporting carried an “overly positive tone,” and that by “not being aware of the true condition of the business,” management had “postponed urgent corrective steps.” He resigned in 2013, citing “fundamental problems.”

    Consider this

    Auditors and audit committees are the system's truth-tellers. What happens to that safeguard when the chief financial officer and the external auditor are this close — and the disclosures stay relentlessly upbeat right up to the collapse?

    The two halves of Failure 5 are really one problem seen twice. The familiarity between an ex-Deloitte-partner CFO and his old firm as auditor is precisely the closeness that auditor-independence rules exist to prevent. And the “overly positive tone” is the provisioning override of Failure 2 viewed from the outside: results that looked healthier than the business actually was. The one director who objected loudly enough — the audit chair — left. The safeguard did not just fail to fire; the person trying to pull the trigger walked out. In the aftermath, investors sued the former directors and Deloitte for over R2bn.

    The lever · audit-committee effectiveness & auditor independence

    Disclosure and audit are governance mechanisms only to the extent they are independent enough to deliver bad news. An audit that is too close to management, and an audit committee whose dissenter resigns, is not a safeguard — it is a formality that lends false comfort right up to the moment the comfort runs out.

    Failure 6 · incentivesWhat was the chief executive paid to do?

    The last failure is the quietest, and it ties the case back to the morning most directly. Kirkinis's salary was modest — about R2.2m in 2012, less than the chief financial officer's. But salary was beside the point. He and a family trust held a large block of African Bank shares, and on that block he earned roughly R286m in dividends over time, worth perhaps R650m at the peak. His wealth, in other words, came from dividends — and dividends come from growth.

    Consider this

    If almost all of the chief executive's wealth flowed from dividends on his shares, did the incentive structure quietly reward exactly the aggressive, undiversified expansion that ultimately sank the bank? Recall the morning's lesson: pay can be a symptom of weak governance, not only a cure for it.

    This is the African Bank version of a result we met this morning in Bertrand and Mullainathan's work on pay for luck (QJE, 2001). There, the danger was a captured compensation committee paying a chief executive for a windfall he did not create. Here the structure is subtler but related: an incentive package, dominated by dividends, that paid the founder most when the bank grew fastest — and a board unwilling to push back on the growth that fed it. Incentives that reward expansion, overseen by a board that defers to the expander, do not merely fail to restrain risk-taking. They subsidise it.

    The lever · executive incentives

    Pay tied to growth, in the hands of a founder no one would challenge, did not align the chief executive with the firm's long-run health — it aligned him with the very strategy that destroyed it. Incentive design is a governance question, and at African Bank the incentives and the deference pointed the same dangerous way.

    Part Three

    The collapse

    Six failures had quietly assembled a machine that could only run in good weather. In 2012 the weather changed, and the machine did what undiversified, wholesale-funded, over-optimistic machines do. It did not wind down. It fell off a cliff.

    The turn came fast. 2012 was the peak: record profit, a share price near R40, the Bank-of-the-Year award. 2013 brought the first loss — R4.2bn — and, alongside it, a reckless-lending case from the regulator. By December 2013 the bank attempted a R5.5bn equity raise, underwritten by Goldman Sachs, in its own words “to save the group from inevitable bankruptcy.” It was not enough. Once a wholesale-funded bank's lenders begin to doubt it, the doubt feeds on itself: funding costs rise, which deepens the losses, which deepens the doubt.

    The week it ended — August 2014

    The end, when it came, took a single week. On 6 August Kirkinis resigned; the same day, ABIL warned of a loss of R7.6bn or more and the need to raise a further R8.5bn — a rights issue that was plainly untenable. On 7 August Ellerines, the furniture business, entered business rescue, South Africa's equivalent of Chapter 11. Between 31 July and 7 August the shares fell more than ninety percent, to under one rand. By mid-month, trading was suspended and the South African Reserve Bank placed African Bank under curatorship — with the pointed clarification, for the bank's three million borrowers, that there would be “no payment holiday”: collections would continue. The founder, by several accounts, kept buying shares as they fell, because “he believed in his narrative,” and lost roughly ninety-five percent of his own net worth in the wreck of the thing he had built.

    The rescue: a “good bank” and a “bad book”

    To save anything at all, the Reserve Bank split African Bank in two. A “good bank” of about R26bn in performing loans was recapitalised — by the Reserve Bank, the government employees' pension fund, and a consortium of the country's banks — and relaunched as an independent African Bank in April 2016, this time with an explicit mandate to diversify beyond its single product. A “bad book” of roughly R17bn in non-performing loans was carved off, renamed Residual Debt Services, and slowly wound down.

    Part Four

    The verdict, and who paid

    The Reserve Bank commissioned a judge-led investigation into the collapse. Its conclusion is the reason this is a governance case and not a fraud case — and it is worth stating in the report's own careful language.

    The Myburgh Report, published in 2016, found no evidence of fraud. What it found instead was that “the business of the bank was conducted negligently”; that the directors had “acted in breach of their fiduciary duty to the bank,” had “not exercised the required care and skill,” and had “not act[ed] in the best interests of the bank.” No one had stolen. Everyone, collectively, had failed to govern. That distinction is the whole pedagogical value of the case: a crime is a story about bad people, and there is little to learn from it; a governance failure is a story about ordinary, well-credentialed, well-intentioned people inside a structure that did not constrain them — and that is a story that repeats.

    The collapse was not a crime. It was a governance failure — which is far more common, and far more worth studying.

    And now the part that makes this an ESG case rather than a finance footnote. Ask who actually absorbed the loss. Some thirteen thousand small, mostly black investors — who had bought into African Bank through community empowerment vehicles named Hlumisa and Eyomhlaba, as employees, former employees, customers, and ordinary savers — lost their entire investments. Vulnerable borrowers faced continued collection on loans that, in many cases, they should never have been granted. And investors sued the former directors and the auditor for over R2bn, in what became a closely watched test of whether directors can be held personally liable for negligence of this kind.

    This is the “S” in ESG

    A governance failure did not merely lose money for sophisticated funds who knew the risks. It destroyed the savings of the financially excluded — the very people the bank had been built to serve — and kept collecting from borrowers who could least afford it. Bad governance is not an abstraction on a scorecard. It is the machinery by which a boardroom's deference becomes a poor family's ruin.

    Synthesis — six failures, one toolkit

    Step back and the case resolves into a single, clean mapping. Each of the six failures is one of the governance levers from the morning, pulled the wrong way. Reading the table from left to right is reading African Bank; reading it from right to left is reading the entire morning lecture.

    Table 2The six failures, each mapped to the governance mechanism it corresponds to. The case is not a new subject; it is this morning's toolkit, observed in the act of breaking.

    And the common thread? Every one of these mechanisms existed. The board had its independent majority, its risk committee, its audit committee, its disclosures, its remuneration structure. The single thing missing — the thing no committee can supply — was a board willing to override the founder when he was wrong. That is the sentence to carry out of the room: governance is behaviour, not structure.

    In closing

    The dark mirror

    Return, finally, to the guess you made at the start. Was it fraud? It was not. Was it a bad strategy, or poor risk management, or a failed board, or bad luck? It was, in a sense, all of them at once — but underneath each of those labels sat the same cause. The strategy went unchallenged, the risk went unowned, the conflict went unseen, the disclosure went unquestioned, and the incentives pushed the wrong way, because the board would not, in the end, govern the man who had built the bank. The platinum strikes were the trigger. The governance was the gun.

    This is why African Bank is the dark mirror of the morning. There, the governance machine — boards, votes, blockholders, incentives — was the thing that could carry environmental and social preferences into corporate action. Here the identical machine, with the identical parts, carried catastrophe instead: a dominant chief executive, a captured board, an unowned risk, a buried conflict, and the savings of thirteen thousand people who trusted that the textbook architecture meant what it appeared to mean. The lesson is not that governance failed because it was absent. It is that governance failed while fully present — on paper.

    Good governance is not paperwork. It is the difference between a bank and a ruin.
    Carry these out of the room

    Seven independent directors sat on that board. Why did none of the mechanisms fire in time? Was this fundamentally a failure of strategy, of risk, or of boardroom courage? The regulator was engaged from 2011 — did regulation fail, or did governance fail first? And the hardest one: should directors be held personally liable for negligence like this, or would that simply empty boards of anyone willing to serve? There is no single right answer. Defend a position — using the morning's framework, now that you have seen it break.
