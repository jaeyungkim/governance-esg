    Summer ESG Module · Postgraduate · A reading

    Governance Factors in ESG Investing

    From the agency problem to the frontier of shareholder democracy.
    Jae Yung Kim29 June 2026≈ 35 min read

    Environmental and social outcomes are not produced by good intentions. They are produced — or blocked — by a machine: the boards, votes, blockholders and markets through which the people who own a company impose their preferences on the people who run it. This reading is about that machine. We build it from its foundations, watch it carry (and leak) ESG preferences, and end at the research frontier, where even the question of who really decides turns out to be unsettled.

    Prologue

    Who actually decides what a company does?

    In May 2021, a hedge fund that almost no one had heard of did something that, on paper, should have been impossible. The fund was called Engine No. 1. It had been founded only months earlier, and it owned about 0.02% of ExxonMobil — roughly one two-thousandth of one percent of one of the largest oil companies on earth. On the strength of that vanishingly small stake, it nominated four outside directors to Exxon's board on an explicit platform: that the company was mismanaging the energy transition and destroying long-run value by ignoring it.

    Before reading on, it is worth committing to a guess, because the answer is genuinely surprising.

    Consider this

    A brand-new fund holding 0.02% of ExxonMobil runs a slate of directors against management's wishes. How many of the twelve board seats do you think it won — none, one, three? Or is the whole exercise hopeless from the start?

    It won three of the twelve seats. There was no new law, no consumer boycott, no hostile takeover, no white knight buying the company. A rounding-error shareholder reshaped the board of an oil major using a single, ordinary instrument: the shareholder vote. What made it work was that the fund did not need its own shares to win. It needed everyone else's. Large institutional owners — including the enormous index funds that hold Exxon precisely because it is in the index — looked at the case and voted with the insurgent.

    That episode is a good way into the subject of this reading, because it cuts against the instinct most people start with. We tend to imagine that what a company does is decided by its managers, or by whoever owns the most shares, or by the law. The Exxon story says something subtler. Environmental and social outcomes — here, a major oil company taking climate risk seriously — did not happen because anyone wished for them. They were produced by a mechanism. Understand the mechanism and you understand when ESG preferences turn into corporate action and when they evaporate into press releases.

    Governance is not a third silo beside E and S. It is the machinery that turns preferences into what the firm actually does.

    This is the thesis that organises everything below. The "G" in ESG is not a separate box to be scored next to environmental and social factors; it is the transmission mechanism through which all preferences — for profit, for the planet, for workers — reach the firm. Good environmental and social outcomes without good governance are a wish. So we will spend our time on the machine: how it is built, how power actually flows through it, where ESG preferences are carried faithfully, and where they are gamed, re-wired, or simply broken.

    A note on how this reading works. At several points I will stop and pose a question — a yes/no, a number, a prediction — and ask you to commit to an answer before continuing. These are not rhetorical. Each marks a place where intuition and evidence diverge, and where a careful empirical paper has something definite to say. The pattern throughout is the same: a question worth arguing about, the evidence that settles it, and a conclusion you can hold onto.

    Part One

    Foundations: the agency problem

    Every tool of corporate governance exists to solve one problem, and the whole field becomes legible once you see it. The problem is that in the modern public corporation, the people who own the firm are not the people who run it.

    The observation is old. In 1932, Adolf Berle and Gardiner Means described what they called the separation of ownership and control. In a large public company, ownership is dispersed across thousands or millions of small shareholders, none of whom owns enough to bother monitoring management closely, while control sits with professional managers who may own very little of the firm themselves. The owners bear the financial consequences of decisions; the managers make the decisions. Nothing guarantees that their interests line up.

    Four decades later, Michael Jensen and William Meckling gave this gap a precise economic vocabulary (JFE, 1976). Whenever one party — an agent — acts on behalf of another — a principal — and their interests differ, the relationship generates agency costs. These come in three kinds, and the entire governance toolkit is an attempt to manage them. There are monitoring costs, borne by owners who pay boards, auditors and analysts to watch managers. There are bonding costs, borne by managers who credibly tie their own hands — taking on debt, accepting pay that is at risk — to promise good behaviour. And there is the residual loss: the value still destroyed despite both, because no amount of monitoring and bonding is ever perfect or free.

    Seen this way, corporate governance has a one-sentence definition, and it is worth memorising because it keeps the whole subject grounded:

    “Corporate governance deals with the ways in which suppliers of finance to corporations assure themselves of getting a return on their investment.”— Shleifer & Vishny, Journal of Finance, 1997

    How do they assure themselves? Through a toolkit of mechanisms that has changed remarkably little in its outline. There are boards of directors, the delegated monitors who can hire and fire the CEO. There is shareholder voting, the instrument of collective choice. There are blockholders, large owners with both the stake and the incentive to monitor — or to discipline by selling. There is the market for corporate control: takeovers and activist investors. There is disclosure, the transparency and audit regime that makes monitoring possible at all. And there are incentives — the design of executive pay. The first two, boards and voting, are where ESG questions are actually decided, so they will get most of our attention. But notice the thread that runs through all six: every one of them ultimately runs on shareholders choosing to use it. The Exxon insurgents used levers two and four — voting and the market for control — and they worked only because other shareholders cooperated.

    Boards: monitors, or rubber stamps?

    The board of directors is the apex control body. It hires, pays and — crucially — can fire the chief executive; it ratifies strategy and oversees risk and financial reporting. Directors owe fiduciary duties of care and of loyalty to shareholders, and outside, "independent" directors are meant to monitor management rather than collude with it. The classic worry is that this is theatre: that boards are captured by the CEOs they are supposed to supervise, rubber-stamping whatever management proposes.

    Modern governance theory has made that picture more interesting. How a board communicates shapes what directors are willing to reveal and therefore what the board can decide (Malenko, RFS 2014). And productive disagreement turns out to be valuable rather than dysfunctional: the credible threat that a board might deadlock can discipline both the CEO and the directors themselves, improving decisions (Donaldson, Malenko & Piacentino, RFS 2020). Whether a particular board genuinely debates climate risk or board diversity — or waves it through — is therefore a question of governance design, not a slogan. Which brings us to the first place where intuition and evidence part company.

    A question · board diversityShould a firm put more women on its board?

    Imagine a board that is ninety percent male, and a proposal to add several women directors. The question divides people instantly, and it is worth being honest about your own answer before we look.

    Consider this

    First: does board gender diversity actually improve firm performance? Second, and more pointedly: would a binding gender quota raise or lower the value of a typical firm?

    The arguments pull both ways. On one side, more diverse boards may monitor more independently, bring a wider range of information and experience, and resist the group-think that lets a powerful CEO get his way. On the other side, a board assembled to satisfy a quota might tokenise its new members or search from an artificially narrow pool — and, most importantly, the firms that choose to add women may already differ in ways that have nothing to do with diversity. That last point is the trap. In raw data, diverse boards do tend to sit atop better firms. But that correlation could simply mean that good, well-run companies are the ones that add women, in which case crediting diversity for their success would be a mistake. To get a causal answer you have to strip the selection out.

    Renée Adams and Daniel Ferreira did exactly this, in what became the standard reference on the question (JFE, 2009). Their first finding is not in dispute and is genuinely useful: diversity changes how boards behave. Women directors have fewer attendance problems, and they appear to raise attendance among the men around them too. They are more likely to sit on the monitoring committees that do the real oversight work — audit, nominating, governance — and less likely to sit on the compensation committee. And on diverse boards, the chief executive's job becomes more precarious when the stock does badly: CEO turnover grows more sensitive to poor performance. Every one of these points in the same direction. More diverse boards monitor more.

    So far, diversity looks like an unambiguous improvement in oversight. The surprise is what happens when the same authors ask the harder question — not how boards behave, but whether diversity raises firm value. Here the answer depends entirely on how carefully you measure it, and watching the estimate move is the whole lesson. Run a simple correlation between board gender diversity and a standard measure of firm value, and the relationship is positive. Add firm fixed effects, which absorb the stable differences between companies, and the sign flips negative. Instrument for diversity to isolate genuine cause from reverse causation, and it becomes strongly negative.

    What should we make of a result that reverses itself as the statistics improve? Not that diversity is bad — that misreads the paper badly. The right reading is that the cheerful positive correlation was largely selection: valuable, well-governed firms were the ones adding women, so the raw number flattered diversity. Once you isolate the causal effect, the average impact on value is roughly zero to negative — and, importantly, it is positive only in firms whose governance was weak to begin with, where extra monitoring is most needed. A blanket quota imposed on already-well-monitored firms can therefore destroy value even as it improves oversight, because those firms did not lack oversight in the first place.

    How do we know the effect actually turns positive when governance is weak? Adams and Ferreira show it with an interaction term. They take the Gompers–Ishii–Metrick governance index — a count of 24 anti-takeover provisions, where a higher value marks a more entrenched, weakly-monitored firm — and multiply it by board diversity (their Table 11). That interaction comes out positive and significant, so diversity's effect on value rises as governance weakens: negative in well-governed firms, positive in poorly-governed ones.

    The conclusion

    Board gender diversity reliably increases monitoring — attendance, oversight-committee membership, sharper discipline of underperforming CEOs. But its average causal effect on firm value is about zero to negative, with benefits concentrated in poorly-governed firms. Diversity is at once a social goal and a governance-quality question; “more is always better” is a slogan, and the evidence replaces it with “it depends on the firm.”

    A question · executive payAre CEOs paid for luck?

    The textbook purpose of executive pay is alignment: tie a manager's wealth to the share price through equity, options and restricted stock, and you give him a reason to act like an owner. But pay can also be the opposite — a symptom of weak governance, the spoils a captured compensation committee hands to the boss. How would we tell these apart? A clean test is to find a situation where the stock moves for reasons the CEO plainly did not cause, and see whether his pay moves with it anyway.

    Consider this

    An oil company's share price doubles. The reason is not strategy or operational brilliance; the world price of oil simply doubled. Does the CEO's pay rise with this windfall — and if so, roughly how much, relative to pay for performance he actually produced?

    Marianne Bertrand and Sendhil Mullainathan answered this with a memorable title — Are CEOs Rewarded for Luck? The Ones Without Principals Are (QJE, 2001). Using oil-price movements as a source of pure luck for oil firms, they compared how strongly pay responds to a dollar of luck versus a dollar of genuine, skill-based performance. The uncomfortable answer is that CEO pay responds to luck about as much as it responds to skill. On the cleanest, market-based measure the two sensitivities are almost identical; on an accounting measure pay responds to luck even more strongly than to performance the manager controlled.

    The title's second clause is the governance punchline. Pay-for-luck is not uniform across firms; it shrinks where someone is actually watching. When a large shareholder sits on the board — a genuine principal, with the stake and the incentive to object — the luck component of pay falls by roughly a quarter to a third, while pay for genuine performance is left untouched. In other words, monitoring does not make managers cheaper across the board; it specifically strips out the part of pay that rewards them for things they did not do.

    The conclusion

    CEOs are paid for luck about as much as for genuine performance — and that luck-pay is precisely what a serious monitor removes, cutting it by roughly 23–33% when a large shareholder is on the board. Executive pay is therefore partly a symptom of weak governance, not merely a cure for it. It is no accident that shareholders later demanded a direct vote on pay — the “say-on-pay” we turn to next.

    Two foundations, then, are now in place. Governance exists because ownership is divorced from control; a toolkit of six mechanisms manages the resulting agency costs; and boards, for all their subtlety, are only as good as the shareholders who stand behind them. That last point is the hinge of the whole subject. If every mechanism ultimately runs on shareholders, then the natural next question is: who are the shareholders of the modern firm, and how do they actually exercise power?

    Part Two

    Shareholders & voting: the heart of the machine

    Ownership of the public corporation has quietly re-concentrated over the last forty years — not back into the hands of founding families, but into a small number of giant institutions. Who holds the votes, and how they choose to cast them, has become one of the central facts of corporate governance.

    The most striking version of this is the rise of the Big Three — BlackRock, Vanguard and State Street — which together now cast a large and fast-growing share of the votes at U.S. public companies. What makes them unusual is that they are mostly index funds. An index fund holds "the market" by construction; it cannot express disapproval of a company by selling, because selling would mean deviating from its benchmark. For an index fund, then, the only available lever is voice — voting and engagement — rather than exit. The owners with the most votes are precisely the owners who cannot walk away.

    This sets up what is sometimes called the stewardship paradox, and it is worth stating as a genuine tension rather than a settled fact. The worry runs like this: index funds hold enormous voting power but have weak incentives to use it well. Their fees are tiny, and any value they create by improving a company is shared with every rival fund that holds the same stock, so why would they spend real money on careful stewardship? Left there, the argument predicts chronic under-investment in monitoring by exactly the owners who matter most.

    The evidence complicates that pessimism. Studying firms pushed into index membership, Appel, Gormley and Keim found that higher passive ownership is associated with more independent directors, fewer takeover defences such as poison pills, and less use of unequal voting structures (JFE, 2016). Their title is the rebuttal in three words: passive investors are not passive owners. They may not pick stocks, but they do appear to use their votes in the direction of stronger governance. Whoever controls these blocks of votes effectively decides a great many ESG questions for the entire market — and the data suggest they are, in fact, voting.

    How a vote actually works — and why timing matters

    The mechanics are worth knowing precisely, because one detail will return to haunt us at the frontier. A company lays out the items to be voted on in a proxy statement (the DEF 14A). A record date then fixes exactly who is eligible to vote — you must own the shares on that day. At the annual meeting the votes are cast, overwhelmingly by proxy and often according to a standing policy rather than a fresh decision, and the results are disclosed afterward. Hold onto the record date. Eligibility is fixed on a single day, but shares trade every day, which means the set of people entitled to vote can differ from the set of people who owned the firm a week earlier or a week later. That gap looks like a technicality. In Part Four it becomes the whole story.

    What do shareholders vote on? Directors, most fundamentally — the core accountability vote. The advisory say-on-pay vote on executive compensation, which is where the pay problem of Part One re-enters as a governance instrument. Routine ratifications and major transactions such as mergers. And, under SEC Rule 14a-8, shareholder proposals, including the environmental and social proposals where ESG meets the ballot directly.

    Blockholders, and a counter-intuitive result about liquidity

    Not every owner is small and passive. A blockholder — a single large owner — can discipline managers in two quite different ways, a distinction Albert Hirschman made famous: by voice, staying and monitoring, or by exit, selling the stock and pushing down its price (Hirschman 1970; Edmans, JF 2009). The intuitive view is that liquid, easily-traded markets are bad for monitoring, because they make it too easy to flee rather than fix. Maug showed the intuition is incomplete (JF, 1998): liquidity can actually help monitoring, because it lets a would-be monitor quietly accumulate a larger stake before acting, and then profit from the improvement he brings about. Liquidity and control are not always a trade-off — a theme that recurs when we reach trading and voting together.

    A question · hedge-fund activismWhen an activist arrives, is value built or stripped?

    The most aggressive users of voice are activist hedge funds. The pattern is familiar: a fund quietly buys a stake of a few percent in an underperforming company, files the disclosure that announces its presence (a Schedule 13D), and demands change — sell a division, replace directors, return cash to shareholders. Public reaction to these campaigns is sharply divided, and the disagreement is empirical, so it can be settled.

    Consider this

    An activist takes a roughly six-percent stake and starts agitating. Over the following year, does the target's stock rise because lazy management is finally disciplined, fall because raiders strip the company for parts, or pop on the news and then revert as the hype fades?

    Brav, Jiang, Partnoy and Thomas assembled the canonical evidence on around a thousand activist targets (JF, 2008). The market greets the announcement with an abnormal return of roughly +7% — and, crucially, that gain does not reverse over the following year, which is what you would expect to see if it were mere overreaction. Underneath the stock move, real things change at the targets: operating performance improves, payouts to shareholders rise, chief executives are more likely to be replaced, and their pay tends to fall. The activists' demands succeed, at least in part, about two-thirds of the time.

    The conclusion

    On average, hedge-fund activism creates value and the gain sticks: targets earn a roughly +7% announcement return with no reversal, alongside real operating, payout and governance improvements. The popular image of the short-termist asset-stripper is not supported in the broad data — though distributional fights, in which gains to shareholders come partly at the expense of workers or creditors, remain a fair and separate concern.

    A question · proxy advisorsHow much does ISS really move a vote?

    A dispersed owner cannot possibly research the thousands of ballot items it votes on each year, so it buys advice. Two firms dominate this market — Institutional Shareholder Services (ISS) and Glass Lewis — and ISS alone covers something like forty thousand meetings in over a hundred countries for more than sixteen hundred institutional clients. The obvious worry is that these advisors have become unelected kingmakers, effectively deciding outcomes for owners who simply follow the recommendation. But how would we measure their influence? You cannot just compare votes that ISS opposed with votes it supported, because ISS opposes the proposals that were troubled to begin with — the correlation is contaminated by everything that made those proposals controversial.

    Consider this

    ISS flips its recommendation on a say-on-pay proposal from for to against. By how many percentage points does shareholder support fall as a result? Is it essentially zero, because funds do their own homework — or huge, because everyone follows ISS? Pick a number.

    Malenko and Shen found a way to isolate the causal effect (RFS, 2016). ISS determines its recommendation partly by a sharp performance cutoff, which means two firms sitting just on either side of that threshold are nearly identical in every respect except that one receives a negative recommendation and the other does not. Comparing across that knife-edge — a regression-discontinuity design — isolates the recommendation's own effect from everything else. The answer is large: a negative ISS recommendation causally reduces say-on-pay support by about 25 percentage points, an estimate that lands between roughly 20 and 29 points across years and specifications.

    That sounds like the kingmaker story confirmed — but the picture is subtler, and the subtlety matters for policy. Most large funds do not actually vote ISS's headline benchmark recommendation; on the order of 80% of them vote a custom policy that ISS helps them implement but that reflects their own preferences. So "the advisor tells everyone how to vote" is overstated. ISS genuinely moves about a quarter of a contested vote, which is real power, but most of its clients are expressing their own policies, not blindly deferring — a distinction that should shape how, and how heavily, we regulate proxy advisors.

    The conclusion

    A negative ISS recommendation causally removes about a quarter of the vote on a contested say-on-pay item — genuine, measurable influence, not mere correlation. But because roughly 80% of funds vote their own custom policies, “ISS decides everything” is an overstatement. The influence is real and bounded at the same time.

    Step back and the shape of Part Two is clear. Voting power has concentrated in a handful of institutions and the advisors who serve them, and those owners — index funds included — do use it. The vote is the channel through which owners' preferences, financial and environmental alike, reach the firm. The natural next question is what happens when the preferences flowing through that channel are specifically about E and S.

    Part Three

    Governance × ESG: where preferences become action — or don't

    Environmental and social preferences reach a company through two channels: engagement behind closed doors, which is voice, and the public ballot, which is voting. In both, the quality of the governance machine decides whether a preference becomes corporate action or merely a press release. This part is about the ways the channel succeeds, leaks, and fails.

    The formal route for an environmental or social preference is the Rule 14a-8 shareholder proposal — on climate, on diversity, on political spending — together with the advisory "say-on-climate" votes on transition plans that spread after 2021. That year was the high-water mark, with the Exxon contest of our prologue as its emblem. Since then, support for environmental and social proposals has in fact fallen, for a tangle of reasons that are still being sorted out: a wave of anti-ESG state legislation, the Big Three stepping back from climate coalitions under political pressure, and a flood of often-vague proposals that depress average support. Whether this is fatigue, capture, backlash, or simply a changing mix of owners is an open empirical question. But the deeper problem is not the level of support; it is that support, as a number, may not mean what it appears to mean.

    A question · strategic votingDo “ESG” funds actually vote their values?

    Consider a fund that markets itself as an environmental and social champion, and a climate proposal coming to a vote. You would expect such a fund to support it. The interesting question is what happens at the margin where its vote actually matters.

    Consider this

    When an ESG fund's vote would genuinely decide the outcome — a close, pivotal vote — does it support the environmental proposal more often than usual, the same, or less?

    The instinct is "more, or at least the same." Michaely, Ordóñez-Calafí and Rubio document the opposite (Review of Finance, 2024). An environmental-and-social fund's extra willingness to support these proposals is large when its vote is unlikely to be pivotal — when supporting is essentially free, a costless signal of virtue. But that extra support collapses by around 60% precisely when the vote is close enough to decide the outcome and the fund's "yes" would actually impose the costs the proposal carries. The pattern is the fingerprint of strategic, rather than sincere, voting.

    The conclusion

    ESG funds back environmental and social proposals when doing so is costless, and quietly withdraw support precisely when their vote would be decisive. Average support therefore overstates true commitment: the headline number is padded with non-pivotal yes-votes. Greenwashing, in other words, shows up not only in brochures but in voting behaviour — which is why behaviour must be read strategically, not at face value.

    A question · engagementDoes quiet engagement pay?

    Voting is the public channel; the private one is engagement — a large investor pressing management behind closed doors, with no proxy fight. Does it accomplish anything measurable for shareholders?

    Consider this

    A large fund engages a company privately on an ESG issue. Does this pay off in the stock always, only when the engagement succeeds, or essentially never? And how often do such engagements actually succeed — one in two, one in five, one in twenty?

    Dimson, Karakaş and Li studied a long series of such engagements (RFS, 2015). Their answer is sharply conditional. A successful engagement is followed by an abnormal return of about +7% over the following year. An unsuccessful one is followed by essentially nothing — no reward, but no penalty either; there is little downside to trying and failing. Averaged across all attempts the effect is a modest +2%, precisely because success is uncommon: only about 18% of engagements succeed overall, and governance-related requests (around 24%) succeed more often than environmental and social ones (around 13%).

    The conclusion

    Engagement pays — but only when it succeeds: about +7% when it works, essentially zero when it does not, and success comes only about one time in five (more often for governance asks than for E&S ones). “Active ownership” creates value, but it is hard, selective work, not a free lunch.

    One caution before we leave this part. That +7% is a statement about shareholder value, and shareholder value is not the same as social welfare. Bond and Levit show that environmental and social commitments interact with market power in non-obvious ways, so that a pro-social pledge is not automatically welfare-improving once a firm's market power is taken into account (JFE, 2025). Good for shareholders need not mean good for society — a distinction that returns, formalised, at the frontier.

    The dark mirror — this afternoon's case
    When the governance machine breaks completely, the consequences are not abstract. The afternoon's case study, African Bank (ABIL) in South Africa, is a study in failure: a captured board, an overconfident chief executive, and a concentrated, high-risk book of unsecured loans to vulnerable borrowers, ending in collapse and severe social fallout. Bad governance does not merely lower returns; it manufactures environmental and social catastrophe. ABIL is the negative image of everything in this reading.

    The governance machine, then, can be gamed (strategic voting), it can pay off only selectively (engagement), and it can break outright (ABIL). In every case the outcome was produced by the machine, not wished into being. Which sets up the final move: from how the machine works to the question of who, in the end, controls it.

    Part Four

    The frontier: shareholder democracy

    We have been treating the electorate of a shareholder vote as fixed — a given set of owners who then express their preferences. The research frontier dissolves that assumption, and with it a good deal of our intuition about what a vote even means.

    The key idea is disarmingly simple once stated. People trade before they vote. Because shares change hands right up to the record date, the set of people who own the firm at the moment of the vote is not handed down from outside; it is itself an outcome, chosen in the market. The electorate is endogenous. This is the subject of a recent paper by Levit, Malenko and Maug, descriptively titled Trading and Shareholder Democracy (JF, 2024), and it is easiest to feel through a small thought experiment before seeing its consequences.

    A thought experiment you can run in a room
    Split a class into two identical rooms, each half pro-change and half status-quo, each person holding a couple of voting shares plus cash and free to buy a few more at a posted price. Tell room A that the proposal will probably pass; tell room B it will probably fail. Let both trade for two minutes, then hold a share-weighted vote. The two rooms — identical in everything but the rumour — routinely reach different outcomes. The expectation did not predict the result; it helped create it, by sorting the shares into the hands of whichever side believed it would win.

    Why does that happen? Because expectations change how much each side values the shares, valuations sort the ownership, ownership determines the vote weights, and the vote then validates the expectation that started the whole loop. Trading and voting reinforce each other, which has three consequences that should genuinely unsettle the standard picture. Outcomes can be self-fulfilling, supporting multiple equilibria for the very same fundamentals. Making trading easier can leave shareholders worse off, by sharpening this sorting. And a high share price need not signal happy owners: in this world, price and welfare come apart.

    A question · the dataAfter a contentious vote, who trades?

    The thought experiment makes a sharp, testable prediction about the real world. If shares sort by belief around a vote, then once a contentious meeting resolves, the people who find themselves on the losing side should no longer wish to hold a company being run against their preferences.

    Consider this

    Once a contentious shareholder meeting is decided, who trades more — the winners or the losers? And in which direction do the losers go?

    Li, Maug and Schwartz-Ziv looked directly at fund trading around shareholder meetings (RFS, 2022). The losers sell. Funds that voted with the losing side systematically reduce their holdings afterward — selling more and buying less — while the winners do not trade unusually at all. The result is that the shareholder base becomes more homogeneous after each contested vote: dissenters leave, like-minded owners remain. Trading volume around the meeting spikes to roughly twenty percent above normal and stays elevated for up to four weeks, even when prices barely move — a telltale that the trading is driven by disagreement among shareholders rather than by news about value.

    The conclusion

    After a contested vote the losers systematically sell and the winners stay, so the electorate re-sorts and the shareholder base becomes more homogeneous. Ownership at the moment of the vote is endogenous — and this post-meeting selling is the empirical fingerprint of exactly the mechanism the thought experiment dramatised. Theory, classroom and data agree.

    Once the electorate is endogenous, even apparently simple questions become deep. What is a vote worth? Levit, Malenko and Maug show that a voting premium can arise purely because blockholders reshape the electorate, with no takeover in sight, and that the premium one measures in market prices does not equal the true value of votes — it can even be negative, when small holders free-ride on a blockholder's effort (The Voting Premium, JF 2026). And the politics reach beyond the firm: when shareholders use corporate "one share, one vote" to push pro-social policy, they can provoke a "one person, one vote" political backlash that undoes the gain, with more diversified investors sometimes intensifying the reaction (Döttling, Levit, Malenko & Rola-Janicka, RFS 2026). The question "who really decides?" turns out to be not rhetorical but formal, and squarely relevant to policy.

    Part Five

    A live question: dual-class “sunset contests”

    We end not with a settled result but with an open one — the question I am working on now, where the machinery of Parts One through Four collides with a fast-spreading corporate structure and the answer is genuinely unknown.

    The structure is the dual-class share. When Snap went public in 2017 it sold shares carrying no votes at all; Google and Meta, like Snap, let their founders keep firm voting control while holding only a small slice of the cash flows. The arrangement forces apart two things that usually travel together, and separating them is the key to thinking clearly about it.

    Consider this

    Snap sold shares with zero votes, whose claim on the company's cash flows is otherwise identical to a normal share. Would you pay more or less for the no-vote share? And, separately: is a founder's voting control good or bad for the value of the firm?

    The two halves of that question have different answers, and seeing why is the heart of the matter. Gompers, Ishii and Metrick used dual-class firms to estimate the two effects separately (RFS, 2010). As insiders hold more cash-flow rights — more genuine ownership, more skin in the game — firm value rises and then flattens: this is the familiar incentive-alignment effect. But as insiders hold more voting rights — more control, holding ownership fixed — firm value falls: this is entrenchment, the value destroyed when managers are insulated from accountability. Ownership aligns; control insulates. The gap between the two — the wedge — is where the danger lives.

    This would be a historical curiosity if dual-class structures were rare, but they are not. They have surged from a low single-digit share of U.S. initial public offerings two decades ago to nearly 30% of IPOs in 2017–2019, a rise driven overwhelmingly by founders rather than by private equity or other insiders (Aggarwal, Eldar, Hochberg & Litov, JFE 2022). And consistent with the wedge logic, firm value rises with a founder's wedge only up to a point — peaking around a 25-point gap and falling thereafter. A little founder control can add value; too much destroys it.

    The conclusion — and the bridge to an open problem

    Insider cash-flow ownership raises value through alignment; insider voting control lowers it through entrenchment. A small founder wedge can help, but past roughly 25 points it destroys value — and the structure now appears in nearly a third of IPOs. So the live policy question is not “ban or allow,” but how to make control expire.

    The sunset, and the contest over it

    The natural fix is a sunset: a provision under which super-voting rights expire after some years or once the founder's stake falls below a threshold. Investors such as ISS and the Council of Institutional Investors press for them; legal scholars Bebchuk and Kastiel argue that the case for founder control weakens as time passes from the IPO, and name three designs — time-based, triggering-event, and ownership-percentage sunsets. The policy fight is wide open; in 2023, S&P even reversed its own ban on adding dual-class firms to its indexes.

    Here is where my own work begins, and it starts from a real and slightly absurd episode. The Trade Desk extended its sunset twice, under two different rules. In 2020 it extended under a majority-of-the-minority vote, in which only unaffiliated holders counted — and it passed. In 2025 it extended again under a combined vote, in which the founder's own super-votes counted toward approval — and it passed again, drawing a Nasdaq reprimand along the way. Same founder, same company, two opposite rules, both producing the same result. So what, exactly, does the rule accomplish?

    The puzzle has a precise structure. Under a combined vote a controlling founder wins almost by definition, so the sunset barely constrains him. The majority-of-minority rule looks like the fix, because it bars the founder from voting his own shares. But it contains a loophole I have been modelling: although the founder cannot vote his shares, he can buy out the very dissenters whose votes would block him — and once bought out, those shares drop out of the protected minority entirely. A rule meant to protect the minority can, in this way, hollow it out from within.

    So the open question is not whether sunsets are a good idea in principle — almost everyone agrees they are — but which design actually disciplines a founder rather than merely appearing to. When does a sunset bind, and which version is hardest to game? That is genuinely unsettled, it is where the formal machinery of this reading points, and it is the question I will put back to you: what do you think?

    In closing

    Five things to carry away

    If the detail fades, these five claims are the skeleton worth keeping. First, governance is the transmission mechanism for environmental and social outcomes — the machine that turns preferences into action, not a third silo beside E and S. Second, that machine exists because ownership is separated from control, which creates the agency problem and the toolkit built to manage it. Third, power now sits with institutions, advisors and the vote — and, contrary to the pessimistic story, they use it: ISS moves about a quarter of a contested vote, and passive funds are not passive owners. Fourth, the electorate itself is endogenous, so voting is subtler than it looks and ESG votes in particular can be quietly gamed. Fifth, the frontier — how to design a dual-class sunset that actually binds — is live and unsolved.

    The reason any of this matters for ESG is the thesis we started with. Environmental and social outcomes are produced by the governance machine or not at all. Learn the machine, and you learn how — and whether — E and S actually get delivered.
